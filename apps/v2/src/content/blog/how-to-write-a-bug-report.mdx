When you start your career in tech, it's easy to obsess over code, frameworks, and syntax. But as you grow, you realize that a huge part of the job is actually communication. Whether it's writing a self-review, giving a status update, or answering behavioral questions, the "soft" skills often determine how effective you are as an engineer as much as your "hard" skills.

I am writing a series of posts to decode these non-coding tasks. My goal isn't just to give you a template to copy-paste, but to explain the _why_ behind them. Once you understand the underlying reasons, the _how_ becomes obvious.

Let's start with one of the most common, yet frequently mishandled tasks: **the bug report**.

## The why of a bug report

Let's consider the purpose of a bug report. Why don't we just post in the group chat about the bug we found? Why take the effort to fill out a template?

The purpose of a formal report is to make sure bugs are tracked and addressed effectively, something a chat message often fails to do.

To achieve that, a bug report should:

- **establish a bug exists in the system.** Anyone reading the report should be able to immediately verify that the system is not behaving as intended.
- **assist the resolution of the bug.** The report should help with resolution of the bug by communicating the bug clearly and include sufficient detail for the responding engineer to fix the issue.
- **provide a paper trail.** The report serves as a historical record for project tracking and future documentation (e.g. documenting that a specific condition was added to handle a quirk in a third-party system).

## Elements of a bug report

To meet its goals effectively, a bug report generally includes the following elements.

### The what: expected vs actual

Before investigating a bug, you must clearly define it. This section clarifies the gap between intention and reality. Explicitly separating these two streamlines the discussion if the bug turns out to be a misunderstanding of the feature.

- **Expected behavior**: What did you think should happen? (e.g. "The list should show all active users.")
- **Actual behavior**: What actually happened? (e.g. "The list was empty")

<Aside>

**Pro Tip:** As you gain experience, describing only the actual behavior is often sufficient. The litmus test is simple: if the Expected Behavior is just the direct opposite of the Actual Behavior, you can skip it.

For example, if the Actual Behavior is "The app crashes on launch," you don't need to write "Expected: The app does not crash." That is implied.

</Aside>

### The proof: visuals and logs

Once you have defined the bug, you need to prove it. The proof captures the moment the bug occurred. It could be a screen recording if UI is involved or terminal output for CLI tools. Proof is vital for three reasons:

- **It minimizes miscommunication caused by different mental models.** Each of us has a specific mental model for the system we interact with. When a bug occurs, reporters often describe the issue based on what they think is relevant (their model), rather than the objective reality. For instance, a user might report "Search is broken" because they didn't realize a "Status: Open" filter was active. They omit the filter from the report because they didn't think it mattered. Proof objectively captures the actual output of the system, instead of the interpretation based on the reporter's mental model.

- **It captures invisible context.** Screenshots and logs often include technical details that the reporter doesn't know are relevant. Common examples are timestamps and subtle UI states (like a spinner that never stops loading). These details allow engineers to narrow down their investigations, for example correlate the report with server-side logs using the timestamps.

- **It prevents the "it works on my machine" dismissal.** If an engineer cannot reproduce the bug, they may assume the reporter is mistaken and close the ticket. However, a screen recording is irrefutable evidence. Even if the engineer's local environment works perfectly, the proof forces them to accept that the failure is real. This shifts the conversation from questioning _if_ the bug exists to investigating _why_ the environments differ.

<Aside>

**Pro Tip:** Avoid cropping your screenshots too tightly. Always capture the **full window or screen**.

While you should highlight the specific part that is relevant in your description, the surrounding context (like the URL bar, visible browser extensions, or the OS clock) often contains valuable information for the responding engineer.

</Aside>

### The how: reproduction steps

Reproduction steps are the specific instructions to make the issue occur again. They are crucial because:

- **They act as interactive proof.** They allow reader to reproduce the bug while tweaking the system (e.g. adding logging) to gather more data.
- **The writing process clarifies your thought.** Trying to find the minimal steps to reproduce a bug forces you to isolate the variables, making your report significantly more accurate.

It's important to include environment details as well, e.g. browser version, operating system, staging/production environment etc. These information is important in case the bug is specific to a certain environment.

#### What if you can't reproduce it?

Sometimes a bug appears without a consistent pattern. In that case, document your _attempts_ to reproduce it and the observed inconsistency. This is still useful data: inconsistency often points to race conditions or timing issues.

### The reference: unique ID

Finally, we need to ensure the bug is easily referenced with a unique ID. Most tools (JIRA, GitHub) generate this automatically, but its value comes from how you use it.

This seemingly simple ID would makes the bug report much more useful because it allows the bug to be referenced in:

- other bug reports that are related to it. For example, a bug report with the same root cause can be linked to this bug by including the ID in that report.
- commit message of the code fix. When engineers inspect the code change based on the commit message, they can find the full context of the bug using the ID.

## Conclusion

A bug report, essentially, is a tool to help the team to resolve bugs effectively by assisting the communications related to the bug. Once you have a clear understanding of the purpose of a bug report, you would be able to determine what information to include in it.
